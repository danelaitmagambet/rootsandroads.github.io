How It Feels to Not Belong Anywhere

A meditation on third culture identity, perpetual foreignness, and finding peace in the space between worlds.

S
By Sena
November 16, 2025 · 6 min read
Feature Image Placeholder

There's a particular kind of loneliness that comes from being too foreign everywhere you go. In Kazakhstan, people ask why my Russian has an accent. In Europe, I'm perpetually explaining where my country is on a map. I exist in the hyphen between cultures, in the pause between languages, in the uncertain space where "home" becomes a question rather than an answer.

I used to think this feeling was temporary—that eventually, I would settle into one identity, one place, one clear sense of belonging. I'm beginning to realize that for people like me, belonging isn't a destination. It's something else entirely.

The Geography of Identity

I was born in Almaty but left when I was eight. I grew up between three countries, attending international schools where everyone was from somewhere else. My friends were Korean-Americans born in Singapore, Turkish kids who'd lived in six countries, British children who'd never actually lived in Britain.

We were third culture kids—formed not by one national culture but by the experience of moving between them. Our shared culture was transition itself, adaptation itself, the particular skills required to be perpetually new.

Now, as an adult, I hold a Kazakh passport but struggle to read Kazakh fluently. I speak Russian at home and English everywhere else. I dream in multiple languages. When people ask where I'm from, I freeze, unsure which answer they're actually asking for.

"Home wasn't a place on a map. It was the feeling of being understood without having to explain yourself first."

The Questions You Learn to Dread

"Where are you from?" seems like such a simple question. For most people, it is. For me, it's a calculation. Do they want to know where I was born? Where I grew up? Where I live now? Where my parents live? Where I feel most at home? These are all different answers.

I've learned to read the context, to gauge what answer will require the least follow-up explanation. Sometimes I say Kazakhstan and brace for the inevitable "Where's that?" Sometimes I name the city I currently live in and accept being misidentified as a local. Sometimes I just say "it's complicated" and change the subject.

What I never say, because it sounds melodramatic, is the truth: I don't know. I genuinely don't know how to answer that question in a way that feels complete and honest.

Neither Here Nor There

When I visit Kazakhstan, I'm treated as slightly foreign. My cousins make gentle fun of my accent. I miss cultural references everyone else my age grew up with. I have to Google Kazakh political figures. I don't instinctively understand certain social codes.

But abroad, I'll always be the Kazakh girl. No matter how many years I live elsewhere, no matter how fluent I become in other languages, my face marks me as Other. People make assumptions about my background, my education, my experiences—assumptions that are sometimes flattering, often inaccurate, always reductive.

I exist in permanent liminal space. Too Westernized for Kazakhstan, too Kazakh for the West. Not quite an insider anywhere, but not exactly an outsider either. I'm perpetually translating—not just between languages, but between cultural contexts, between different versions of myself.

"I learned that you can be homesick for a place you've never fully belonged to, nostalgic for a version of yourself that only existed in transition."

The Exhaustion of Explanation

Every new person I meet requires the same introductory conversation. Explaining my name. Explaining my background. Explaining why I don't look like what they expected, why I don't sound like what they assumed, why my story doesn't fit the simple narrative they were hoping for.

I've become efficient at this. I have a two-minute version and a ten-minute version and a version for when someone really wants to understand. I've learned which details to include, which to leave out, how to make my complicated history digestible for strangers.

But I'm tired of being interesting. I'm tired of my identity being a story I have to tell. Sometimes I just want to exist without explanation, to be somewhere where people already understand the context of who I am.

Finding Community in Displacement

The irony is that the people who understand me best are other people who don't quite belong anywhere. We recognize each other instinctively—in the slight pause before answering "where are you from," in the ease with which we switch between languages mid-sentence, in the way we relate to places as temporary rather than permanent.

We're our own country, in a way. A scattered nation of third culture individuals who share the experience of multiplicity, of living between fixed categories, of making home portable.

These friendships are precious because they require less translation. We understand each other's references to being caught between cultures. We don't need to explain the particular loneliness of video calls with family in different time zones, or the disorientation of not having a simple answer to simple questions.

The Unexpected Gifts

Not belonging anywhere has its compensations, though I wouldn't have recognized them as gifts when I was younger and desperately wanted to just fit in somewhere.

I'm comfortable with ambiguity in ways that people with more rooted identities sometimes aren't. I can see multiple perspectives on the same situation. I'm adaptable, resilient, able to find my footing in unfamiliar contexts relatively quickly.

I speak multiple languages not just literally but culturally. I can code-switch, adjust my behavior and communication style to different contexts. This isn't performing or being fake—it's genuine multilingual identity.

I've learned that identity doesn't have to be singular or stable. I can be many things simultaneously without any of them being less real. I can love multiple places, carry multiple cultural influences, hold seemingly contradictory values in productive tension.

"Perhaps not belonging anywhere means you have the freedom to belong everywhere, to create home wherever you are rather than waiting to find it."

Making Peace with In-Between

I'm slowly learning to stop seeing my in-between-ness as a problem to be solved. For years, I thought I needed to choose—to commit fully to being Kazakh or to assimilate completely into wherever I was living. To pick a side, plant roots, stay still long enough to finally belong.

But that's not who I am. I'm not meant to fit neatly into one national category. My identity is genuinely hybrid, formed by movement and multiplicity. That's not a failure of belonging—it's a different kind of belonging.

I belong to languages, to ideas, to people, to experiences. I belong to the community of perpetual outsiders. I belong to the space between fixed points, to the journey rather than the destination.

A Different Definition of Home

I've had to redefine home. It's not a place anymore—or not just a place. Home is anywhere I can be fully myself without extensive explanation. Home is the friends who understand my references to multiple cultures. Home is the moment when someone gets my particular sense of humor that's been shaped by growing up between languages.

Home is in my mother's voice on the phone, in the Kazakh language I speak imperfectly but love deeply, in the books I carry from country to country, in the playlist that mixes music from every place I've lived.

Home is portable, multiple, flexible. It's something I create rather than something I find.

To Others Living Between

If you're reading this and recognizing yourself in these words, I want you to know: your complicated identity is valid. You don't have to choose. You don't have to simplify yourself to make others comfortable. You don't have to fit neatly into checkboxes that weren't designed for people like us.

The discomfort of not quite belonging anywhere is real, and it's okay to grieve for the simple rootedness you might never have. But there's also beauty in your multiplicity, strength in your ability to navigate between worlds, wisdom in your expanded perspective.

You are not lost. You are not broken. You are not failing at identity. You're just complex in ways that simple categories can't capture.

And maybe that's exactly as it should be.
