Is It Really Worth Studying Abroad?

An honest reflection on the promises, realities, and hidden costs of international education—beyond the glossy brochures and Instagram photos.

M
By Maria
November 17, 2025 · 9 min read
Feature Image Placeholder

Every year, thousands of Kazakh students apply to universities abroad, dreaming of world-class education, global networks, and transformative experiences. We're told that studying abroad is an investment in our future, a ticket to better opportunities, a chance to "find ourselves" in foreign lands. But is it really worth it?

I'm writing this from my apartment in London, three years into a degree that has cost more than my parents' combined salaries for a decade. I've gained things I never could have imagined—and lost things I didn't know I valued. This isn't a story with a simple yes or no answer. It's more complicated than that.

The Dream vs. The Reality

The promotional materials for international universities sell a specific vision: diverse classrooms, intellectual debates with brilliant peers, professors who are leaders in their fields, career opportunities that will change your life trajectory. And to be fair, some of this is true.

What they don't show you is the 3 AM anxiety attacks when you're wondering if this degree will actually be worth the debt. The isolation of being the only person in your cohort who has to explain where Kazakhstan is on a map. The exhaustion of code-switching between languages, cultures, and identities multiple times a day.

"Education abroad promises transformation, and it delivers—but not always the kind of transformation you expected or wanted."

The Financial Reality Check

Let's talk about money, because nobody wants to. International tuition fees are astronomical. Even with scholarships, the cost of living in major cities can drain your savings faster than you ever anticipated. You calculate exchange rates in your head constantly. You skip social events because you can't afford another restaurant meal. You feel guilty every time your parents transfer money.

The financial burden isn't just about numbers in a bank account. It's psychological weight. It's the pressure to make every moment "count," to justify the investment, to succeed spectacularly enough that the sacrifice feels worth it. It's the fear that if you struggle, if you're unhappy, if you want to quit—you've wasted everyone's money and faith in you.

The Hidden Costs

Beyond tuition and rent, there are costs you never budgeted for. Visa applications and renewals. Healthcare that isn't covered by your insurance. Flights home that become more expensive during the holidays when you actually want to travel. The opportunity cost of not working full-time in Kazakhstan, not building professional networks at home, not being present for family milestones.

What You Actually Learn

Here's the thing though: I have learned an incredible amount. Not all of it came from classrooms, and much of it wasn't in the course catalog.

I learned resilience. How to navigate bureaucratic systems in a second language. How to build community from scratch in a place where you know no one. How to advocate for yourself when professors mispronounce your name or make assumptions about your background. How to find strength in discomfort.

I learned critical thinking that challenges everything I thought I knew. My professors pushed me to question assumptions, analyze from multiple perspectives, support arguments with evidence. This intellectual rigor has fundamentally changed how I engage with the world.

I learned about my own culture by seeing it from the outside. Distance gave me perspective on Kazakhstan that I never had when I lived there. I became a better ambassador for my country because I had to articulate what makes it special to people who'd never heard of it.

"The most valuable education abroad isn't what you learn in lectures—it's what you learn about yourself when everything familiar is stripped away."

The Career Question

Will studying abroad guarantee a better career? Honestly, it depends. An international degree opens certain doors, particularly in multinational companies and organizations. It signals things to employers: adaptability, language skills, cross-cultural competence, ambition.

But it's not a magic credential. I know people with degrees from top universities who struggled to find jobs. I also know people who stayed in Kazakhstan, attended local universities, and built impressive careers through networking, internships, and hard work.

The real career advantage of studying abroad isn't the university name on your diploma—it's the skills you develop along the way. The ability to work in multicultural teams. The confidence to navigate unfamiliar situations. The expanded worldview that helps you think creatively about problems.

The Personal Transformation

Perhaps the most significant impact of studying abroad has nothing to do with academics or careers. It's about who you become in the process.

You develop independence you never knew you needed. You learn to make decisions without your family's immediate input. You discover aspects of your personality that only emerge when you're removed from familiar contexts and expectations.

You also develop a more nuanced understanding of identity. You're forced to confront questions about belonging, culture, and home that people who never leave their birth countries might not have to examine as deeply.

What I Wish I'd Known Before

If I could go back and advise my younger self before making this decision, here's what I'd say:

  • Be honest about your motivations. Are you running away from something or running toward something? The answer matters.
  • Research beyond rankings. University prestige is one factor, but also consider cost of living, support services for international students, alumni networks in Kazakhstan, and actual program content.
  • Have a backup plan. What if you hate it? What if your financial situation changes? It's okay to have an exit strategy.
  • Build community intentionally. Loneliness is the biggest threat to your mental health and academic success. Invest in relationships from day one.
  • Stay connected to home. Don't try to completely reinvent yourself. Your roots matter, even as you grow new branches.

So... Is It Worth It?

For me, personally, yes. But that answer comes with qualifications, caveats, and acknowledgment of privilege. I had family support, scholarships, and circumstances that made this path possible. Not everyone does.

Studying abroad has been one of the most challenging and rewarding experiences of my life. It's made me more independent, more aware, more capable. But it's also been lonely, expensive, and sometimes unbearably hard.

The real question isn't whether studying abroad is worth it in general—it's whether it's worth it for you, given your specific goals, resources, and circumstances. There are many paths to success, growth, and fulfillment. International education is one path, but it's not the only path, and it's not automatically the best path for everyone.

"Worth is not universal. What matters is whether this particular sacrifice, this particular challenge, this particular transformation aligns with your values and aspirations."

Final Thoughts

If you're considering studying abroad, do it with your eyes open. Research thoroughly. Ask hard questions. Talk to people who've done it—not just the ones whose Instagram feeds make it look perfect, but the ones who'll tell you about the struggles too.

Calculate the real costs, both financial and personal. Think about what you're hoping to gain and whether there might be alternative paths to similar outcomes. Be honest with yourself about your resilience, your support systems, and your capacity for uncertainty.

And if you do decide to go, go fully. Embrace the discomfort. Learn from the struggles. Build meaningful connections. Stay curious. Let the experience change you, but don't lose yourself in the process.

Studying abroad isn't just an educational decision—it's a life decision. Treat it with the seriousness and thoughtfulness it deserves.
