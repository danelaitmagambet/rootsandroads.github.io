From Almaty to New York: Finding Home Abroad

Moving to the US was both terrifying and exciting. The hardest part wasn't the language—it was missing the smell of baursak in the morning and learning to build a new kind of home.

A
By Aidar K.
December 10, 2025 · 8 min read
Feature Image Placeholder

The first morning I woke up in New York City, I reached instinctively for my phone to call my mom. Then I remembered: it was 2 AM in Almaty. This simple arithmetic—adding or subtracting hours, calculating when it was appropriate to miss someone—became the rhythm of my new life.

I arrived in August 2022 with two suitcases, a scholarship letter, and the kind of naive confidence that comes from never having truly been alone. I was 18 years old and convinced that adapting to New York would be my greatest adventure. I wasn't wrong, but I also had no idea what "adapting" would actually require.

The Language Paradox

Everyone warned me about the language barrier. I'd studied English since childhood, aced my TOEFL, and felt prepared for academic discussions. What I wasn't prepared for was the loneliness of understanding every word while missing every subtext.

I could parse complex sentences about political theory, but I couldn't catch the joke that made my entire seminar laugh. I understood the literal meaning of "let's hang out sometime" but not that it wasn't actually an invitation. I knew all the words to describe homesickness but couldn't find the ones to explain the specific ache of missing places and people who existed in a language my new friends didn't speak.

"Fluency isn't just vocabulary. It's knowing which silence is comfortable and which is awkward, which joke will land and which will require too much explanation."

The hardest part wasn't speaking English—it was that English couldn't contain everything I needed to express. Some feelings only existed in Russian or Kazakh. Some jokes only made sense if you'd grown up in Almaty. Some comfort only came from conversations that didn't require translation.

The Small Things You Don't Expect to Miss

Before I left, people asked what I'd miss most about Kazakhstan. I gave predictable answers: my family, my friends, the mountains. All true, but incomplete.

What actually broke my heart in unexpected moments: the smell of baursak frying on Saturday mornings. The specific way my grandmother said my name. Hearing Kazakh or Russian spoken casually on the street. Markets where vendors spoke to you like family. Knowing instinctively how to navigate social situations without conscious thought.

I missed beshbarmak so intensely that I tried making it in my dorm's shared kitchen. It tasted wrong with American ingredients, and I cried over a pot of incorrectly spiced meat like it was a profound personal failure. My roommate found me there and tried to be comforting, but how could I explain that this wasn't really about food?

Building Community from Scratch

New York has eight million people, but I'd never felt so alone. In Almaty, I had friends I'd known since childhood, family dinners every weekend, a built-in social structure. Here, I had to construct community from nothing.

I found other international students first—not because we had much in common beyond displacement, but because they understood the particular exhaustion of always performing cultural translation. We bonded over shared confusion about American social norms, over missing foods we couldn't easily find, over the specific loneliness of video calls home.

Slowly, I built connections with American students too. They taught me about Thanksgiving traditions and introduced me to music I'd never heard. Some became genuine friends who asked thoughtful questions about Kazakhstan, who wanted to understand rather than just be entertained by exotic stories.

The Kazakh Diaspora Network

Finding other Kazakhs in New York felt like discovering oxygen in space. Through Facebook groups and university networks, I connected with a small but vibrant Kazakh community. We organized dinners where we spoke Russian without translation, celebrated Nauryz together, shared tips for navigating American bureaucracy.

These gatherings became sacred. We could be fully ourselves without explanation, make references everyone understood, complain in our native languages. It wasn't home, but it was home-adjacent—a vital reminder that we weren't alone in our displacement.

The Education I Didn't Sign Up For

I came to New York for a university education, and I got that. But the real education happened outside classrooms.

I learned to advocate for myself in ways I never had to in Almaty. When professors mispronounced my name, I had to decide whether to correct them or let it go. When assignments included assumptions about "universal" American experiences, I had to speak up about different perspectives.

I learned independence not by choice but by necessity. There was no family to fall back on when things went wrong, no familiar systems to navigate instinctively. Every problem—from understanding health insurance to finding an apartment for my second year—required figuring things out alone.

I learned resilience. The first semester was brutal—academically challenging, socially isolating, emotionally draining. I called my mom crying multiple times, seriously considering giving up and going home. But I didn't. I pushed through, and somewhere in that pushing, I discovered strength I didn't know I had.

"Homesickness doesn't go away. You just get better at carrying it."

Becoming a Bridge

An unexpected responsibility emerged: representing Kazakhstan to people who'd never heard of it. I became an informal ambassador, answering endless questions, correcting misconceptions, explaining our history and culture.

Sometimes this felt empowering—I was introducing people to the richness of Kazakh culture, challenging their limited worldviews. Other times it felt exhausting—I was just one person, not a comprehensive encyclopedia of Central Asian geopolitics.

But I took the responsibility seriously. Every conversation was an opportunity to complicate someone's understanding of the world, to make Kazakhstan real and three-dimensional rather than just a name on a map they couldn't locate.

Redefining Home

Three years in, I still don't have a simple answer to "where is home?" Almaty is home—it's where my family lives, where my childhood memories are rooted, where I feel most fundamentally myself. But New York is also becoming home—it's where I've grown up, built community, created a life.

I've stopped trying to choose. I'm learning to hold both truths simultaneously: I can miss Almaty desperately while also loving my life in New York. I can be homesick for Kazakhstan while being grateful for opportunities I have here. I can be Kazakh and global, rooted and displaced, belonging to multiple places at once.

Home is my mom's voice on the phone at odd hours. Home is the Kazakh restaurant in Brooklyn where the owners speak to me in Russian. Home is my apartment in Manhattan where I've hung photos of Almaty. Home is the friends who've learned to pronounce my name correctly and ask genuine questions about where I'm from.

What I'd Tell My Younger Self

If I could go back to that 18-year-old boarding a plane to New York, I'd tell him: it's going to be harder than you think, and you're going to be okay anyway.

The loneliness is real. The cultural dislocation is disorienting. The homesickness doesn't fade—you just develop more capacity to carry it. But you'll also discover resilience, build meaningful community, and grow in ways that staying comfortable never would have allowed.

You'll learn that you can be homesick and happy simultaneously. You'll become fluent not just in English but in code-switching, cultural translation, and navigating between worlds. You'll carry Almaty with you everywhere, not as a burden but as a foundation.

And you'll learn that finding home abroad doesn't mean losing your original home. It means expanding your definition of home to include both the place that made you and the places that continue shaping you.

"Home isn't singular. It's plural, complex, evolving—like us."
